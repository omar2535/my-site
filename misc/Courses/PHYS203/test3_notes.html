# Test 3 study notes

Notes to study for the third test of this course

# Definitions

  • Latent heat: Heat required to convert a solid into liquid or liquid into gas without a change in temperature.
  • Macrostate: A state of the system specified by macroscopic quantities
  • Microstate: An exact microscopic description of the system, for example, a list of the positions and velocities of all particles in a gas.
  • Multiplicity: Ω\OmegaΩ is the number of microstates per macrostate
    • S=kln⁡ΩS = k \ln\OmegaS=klnΩ
  • Fundamental Assumption of Statistical Mechanics: In an isolated system with fixed internal energy UUU, fixed volume VVV, and fixed number of atoms NNN, then all microstates are equally likely.
    • This imples that an isolated system always evolve towards a macrostate with the largest multiplicity
  • Quasistatic: Keeping the system in equilibrium with itself as the process happens
    • This is the same as keeping the system close to maximum entropy for a given macrostate at all times
    • Is generally slow and careful
  • Temperature: Part of system that tells how much heat it is throwing off.
    • Is related to how much entropy it loses when it throws off heat
    • T=(∂S∂U∣V,N)−1=∂U∂S∣V,NT = \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial U}|_{V,N}\right)^{-1} = \frac{\partial U}{\partial S}|_{V,N}T=(∂U∂S​∣V,N​)−1=∂S∂U​∣V,N​
  • Pressure: Related to how much entropy system gains when volume is increased
    • P=T∂S∂V∣U,NP = T\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}|_{U,N}P=T∂V∂S​∣U,N​
  • Thermodynamic identity: dU=−PdV+TdSdU = -PdV + TdSdU=−PdV+TdS
  • Hemlholtz free energy: F=U−TSF = U - TSF=U−TS
    • Extensive state function
    • For reversible processes, Q=TΔSQ = T\Delta SQ=TΔS
      • F must stay the same if process is reversible
    • For irreversible processes, Q<TΔSQ < T\Delta SQ<TΔS since new entropy is created spontaneously
      • F must decrease if the process is irreversible
      • Implies that increase in free energy F cannot be larger than work done on system
    • ΔF=ΔU−TΔS\Delta F = \Delta U - T\Delta SΔF=ΔU−TΔS
    • ΔU=Q+W\Delta U = Q + WΔU=Q+W
    • ΔF=Q+W−TΔS\Delta F = Q + W - T\Delta SΔF=Q+W−TΔS
  • Enthalpy: H=U+PVH = U + PVH=U+PV
    • ΔH=ΔU+Δ(PV)\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta (PV)ΔH=ΔU+Δ(PV)
    • ΔH=Q−W+ΔPV+PΔV\Delta H = Q - W + \Delta P V + P\Delta VΔH=Q−W+ΔPV+PΔV
    • ΔH=Q−PΔV+ΔPV+PΔV\Delta H = Q - P \Delta V + \Delta P V + P\Delta VΔH=Q−PΔV+ΔPV+PΔV
    • ΔH=Q+ΔPV\Delta H = Q + \Delta P VΔH=Q+ΔPV
  • Gibb's free energy: G=H−TSG = H - TSG=H−TS
    • G=U+PV−TSG = U + PV - TSG=U+PV−TS
    • G=F+PVG = F + PVG=F+PV
    • Phase with lower gibb's free energy is more stable
  • Chemical Potential: Chemical potential μ≡−T∂S∂N∣U,V\mu \equiv -T \frac{\partial S}{\partial N} |_{U,V}μ≡−T∂N∂S​∣U,V​
    • Particles flow from high potential to low potential
    • μ\muμ is an intensive quantity
    • Relevant when N is not constant
    • G=NμG = N\muG=Nμ
  • Clausius-Clapeyron equation: dPdT=LTΔV\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\Delta V}dTdP​=TΔVL​
    • Can predict change in melting pressure when applying extra pressure if we know volume and latent heat of melting.
      • When one of the substance is gas, a useful approximation is ΔV≈V(gas)≈RTP\Delta V \approx V(gas) \approx \frac{RT}{P}ΔV≈V(gas)≈PRT​

    • Vapour equation: T−T0=RT02P0L(P−P0)T - T_0 = \frac{RT_0^2}{P_0L}(P-P_0)T−T0​=P0​LRT02​​(P−P0​)

# Throttling

A throttling process is a process where there is no enthalpy change between states, no work is done, and the process is adiabatic. It is fundamentally irreversible

Ex. 1kg of liquid water at its boiling point at 1atm is throttled so that its temperature is lowered to 20oC20^o\text{C}20oC. What fraction of water is vaporized?

Ans: Knowing that enthalpy of water Hwater=419H_{water} = 419Hwater​=419 at 100 degrees, then the enthalpy must be the same since it is a throttled process. Let xxx be the unknown vaporized water. Hwater=(1−x)Hwater+HsteamH_{water} = (1-x)H_{water} + H_{steam}Hwater​=(1−x)Hwater​+Hsteam​
